Professor Wangari Maathai

A renowned Kenyan environmentalist, political activist, and Nobel laureate.

Here is a timeline of Professor Wangari Maathai's life:

  • 1940 - Professor Wangari Maathai was born on April 1, 1940, in Nyeri, Kenya. She studied biology in the United States, earning a Bachelor of Science degree from Mount St. Scholastica College in Kansas and later obtaining a Master of Science degree from the University of Pittsburgh.
  • 1977 - Professor Wangari Maathai founded the Green Belt Movement, an environmental organization focused on tree planting, conservation, and women's rights. The movement aimed to address issues such as deforestation, soil erosion, and lack of access to resources, while also empowering women through employment and community development.
  • Professor Wangari Maathai was a vocal advocate for environmental conservation and sustainable development in Kenya and beyond. She campaigned against deforestation, land degradation, and the negative impacts of development projects on local communities and ecosystems.
  • 2002-2007 - Professor Wangari Maathai was an active participant in Kenyan politics and civil society. She served as a Member of Parliament in Kenya from 2002 to 2007 and held various government positions, including Assistant Minister for Environment and Natural Resources.
  • 2004 - Professor Wangari Maathai was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize for her contribution to sustainable development, democracy, and peace. She was the first African woman to receive the Nobel Peace Prize.
  • Professor Wangari Maathai's legacy extends beyond her environmental activism. She was a symbol of courage, resilience, and empowerment, particularly for women and marginalized communities. Her work continues to inspire individuals and organizations around the world to take action on environmental issues and social justice.
  • Professor Wangari Maathai authored several books, including her memoir "Unbowed" and "The Challenge for Africa," in which she addressed environmental challenges, governance issues, and the importance of sustainable development in Africa.
  • She remains an iconic figure in the global environmental movement and a symbol of hope for a more sustainable and equitable future.
  • The Green Belt Movement planted more than 30 million trees in Africa and helped about 900,000 women.
  • 2011 - Professor Wangari Maathai passed away from ovarian cancer
